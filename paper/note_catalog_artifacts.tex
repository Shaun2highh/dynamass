\title{Dynamical Stability Audits Expose Three Classes of Parameter Artifacts in Exoplanet Catalogs}

\author{Shaurya Ranjan}
\affiliation{Independent Researcher}
\email{shauryaranjan911@gmail.com}

\section*{}

Aggregated exoplanet catalogs --- above all the NASA Exoplanet Archive ---
are the de facto input for population studies, dynamical analyses, and
target selection. During a homogeneous $N$-body stability survey of
multi-planet radial-velocity systems, we found that requiring a system's
own long-term survival is a sharp, inexpensive audit of its published
parameters: configurations drawn directly from the catalog sometimes
self-destruct within $10^3$--$10^4$ orbits, impossible for systems
observed to be Gyr old. Tracing every such contradiction to its root
exposed three recurring artifact classes. None is hypothetical; each
broke a real analysis before being diagnosed.

\textbf{Class 1: eccentricity point estimates that are dynamically fatal
noise.} Compact low-mass systems are typically fit with small, poorly
constrained eccentricities whose posteriors are consistent with zero, yet
the catalog records nonzero point values. Barnard's star is the extreme
case: its four sub-Earth planets (periods 2.3--6.7~d) carry catalog
eccentricities $e = 0.03$--$0.08$ with $1\sigma$ uncertainties of the
same size (e.g., $e = 0.040 \pm 0.040$). Integrated as written, the
system destroys itself through close encounters within
1{,}400--4{,}300~yr in every realization; the same entry forced circular
survives $10^6$ inner orbits in every realization
(Figure~\ref{fig:barnard}). Neither extreme is correct: drawing
eccentricities from their published uncertainties (truncated at zero)
yields intermediate survival and propagates the ignorance honestly. For
dynamically packed systems, catalog eccentricity point values must be
treated as draws from a distribution, never as facts; stability
conclusions computed from point values alone can be qualitatively wrong
in either direction.

\textbf{Class 2: composite tables mix parameters from incompatible
fits.} The Archive's \texttt{pscomppars} table assembles each parameter
from the best available reference per column, so a single system's rows
can combine periods from one paper with eccentricities, periastron
angles, and periastron times from others. For resonant or near-resonant
systems the damage is acute: orbital phases assembled across references
imply precise timing relationships that no fit ever measured. In our
survey, phases derived from composite periastron times falsely collapsed
the stable systems 47~UMa and HD~141399 to near-total instability at
their minimum masses. Phase-sensitive dynamics requires self-consistent
parameter sets from a single reference (the \texttt{ps} table filtered to
one \texttt{pl\_refname}); composite rows are safe only when phase
information is discarded and parameters are sampled within uncertainties.

\textbf{Class 3: fit epochs stored as times of periastron.} For some
references the Archive's \texttt{pl\_orbtper} column contains the
\emph{epoch of the fit} rather than each planet's periastron passage
time. GJ~876 \citep{Rivera2010} is reported with \texttt{pl\_orbtper}
$= 2450602.093$ for all four planets --- the fit epoch --- which, read
literally, places every planet at periastron simultaneously. Phases
derived from these values initialize the celebrated 2:1 Laplace resonance
far from its libration center, and the reconstructed system --- stable in
every published analysis --- is destroyed within 5{,}000~yr even under an
exact (IAS15) integration. The correct mean anomalies ($355\arcdeg$,
$294.59\arcdeg$, $325.7\arcdeg$, $335\arcdeg$ at that epoch;
\citealt{Rivera2010}, their Table~3) restore libration and long-term
stability. Identical periastron times across a multi-planet system are a
reliable signature of this artifact and should be rejected before any
phase information is used.

\textbf{Recommendations.} (1) Treat catalog eccentricities of compact
systems as distributions bounded below by zero, and marginalize stability
conclusions over them. (2) Never derive orbital phases from composite
tables; use single-reference parameter sets for phase-sensitive dynamics.
(3) Reject periastron times that are identical across planets. (4)
Archives could flag columns whose values are epochs rather than
measurements, and offer a dynamically self-consistent single-reference
view. Each check costs seconds; the failures they prevent masquerade
convincingly as science.

All integrations used \textsc{rebound} \citep{ReinLiu2012} with the
WHFast integrator \citep{ReinTamayo2015}, instability defined by
Hill-sphere overlap or escape. Code and per-run records are available at
[GITHUB-URL]. The NASA Exoplanet Archive (\texttt{pscomppars} and
\texttt{ps} tables) was accessed 2026 July 9--13; we thank its
maintainers, whose stewardship makes audits like this possible.
Artificial-intelligence assistance (Claude, Anthropic) was used for code
development and manuscript drafting; all results were independently
verified and are the author's responsibility.
